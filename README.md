## Electric Charge

- Electric charge is a basic property of matter due to which it experiences a force in an electric field.
- It is a **scalar quantity**.
- Two types of charge — **Positive Charge** and **Negative Charge**
- Proton → Positive charge | Electron → Negative charge
- **Basic Law** → Like charges **repel**, unlike charges **attract**.
- **SI Unit** → Coulomb (C)
- Charge on electron = **−1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C**
- Charge on proton = **+1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C**
- This value is called **elementary charge → e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C**
- **1 Coulomb = charge of 6.25 × 10¹⁸ electrons**

---

## Conductors and Insulators

| Conductor | Insulator |
|-----------|-----------|
| Charge flows easily through it | Charge cannot flow through it |
| Has large number of free electrons | Electrons are tightly bound to atoms |
| Examples: Copper, Silver, Aluminium, Iron | Examples: Glass, Rubber, Plastic, Wood |

---

## Properties of Electric Charge

### 1. Additivity of Charge
- The total charge of a system is the **algebraic sum** of all individual charges.
- Sign (+ or −) must be considered.
- **Example:** Charges +3C, −2C, +1C → Total Q = +3 + (−2) + 1 = **+2 C**

---

### 2. Conservation of Charge
- **Statement:** Electric charge can neither be created nor destroyed. It can only be transferred from one body to another. The total charge of an isolated system always remains constant.

**Example 1 — Glass rod and Silk cloth:**
- Glass rod is rubbed with silk → Glass loses electrons → Glass becomes **+ve**, Silk becomes **−ve**
- Total charge before rubbing = 0, Total charge after rubbing = 0 


---

### 3. Quantisation of Charge
- Charge always exists as an **integer multiple of elementary charge e**.

$$\boxed{Q = ne}$$

where n = 0, ±1, ±2, ±3, ... and e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C

- Charges like 0.5e or 1.3e are **not possible in nature**.

**Examples:**
- Charge of 3 electrons = −3e = −3 × 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ = **−4.8 × 10⁻¹⁹ C**
- Charge of 2 protons = +2e = +2 × 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ = **+3.2 × 10⁻¹⁹ C**



---

## Methods of Charging a Body

### 1. Charging by Friction
- Two bodies are rubbed against each other → electrons transfer from one to the other.
- Body that **loses electrons** → becomes **+ve charged**
- Body that **gains electrons** → becomes **−ve charged**
- Total charge of the system remains zero (only transfer, not creation).
- **Example:** Glass rod rubbed with silk cloth.

---

### 2. Charging by Conduction
- A charged body is brought in **direct physical contact** with a neutral body.
- Charge flows until both bodies reach the same potential.
- **Both bodies acquire the same sign of charge.**
- **Example:** A +ve charged metal sphere is touched to a neutral sphere → neutral sphere also becomes **+ve**.

---

### 3. Charging by Induction
- A charged body is brought **near** a neutral conductor **without touching it**.
- Near side of conductor → acquires **opposite charge**
- Far side of conductor → acquires **same sign of charge**
- **The inducing body does not lose any of its charge.**

**Step-by-step Process:**

| Step | What Happens |
|------|-------------|
| Step 1 | Bring +ve charged rod near neutral metal ball (do not touch) |
| Step 2 | Free electrons in ball move toward rod → Near side: −ve, Far side: +ve |
| Step 3 | Connect far side to earth → free electrons from earth flow into the ball and neutralise the +ve charge on far side |
| Step 4 | Remove ground connection first, then remove the rod |
| Step 5 | Ball is left with a net **−ve charge** |

> **Key Difference to Remember:**
> - Conduction → Both bodies get **same sign** of charge
> - Induction → Body gets **opposite sign** of charge

## Coulomb's Law

### Statement:
The electrostatic force between two stationary point charges is **directly proportional to the product of their magnitudes** and **inversely proportional to the square of the distance** between them. This force acts along the straight line joining the two charges.

### Mathematical Derivation:

Let two point charges q₁ and q₂ be placed at distance r in vacuum.

F ∝ q₁q₂ ...(i)

F ∝ 1/r² ...(ii)

Combining both:

$$\boxed{F = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}}$$

$$\boxed{F = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}}$$

**Values:**
- k = Coulomb's constant = **9 × 10⁹ N m² C⁻²**
- ε₀ = Permittivity of free space = **8.85 × 10⁻¹² C² N⁻¹ m⁻²**

---

### Nature of Force:
- q₁q₂ > 0 (same sign charges) → Force is **Repulsive**
- q₁q₂ < 0 (opposite sign charges) → Force is **Attractive**

---

### Vector Form of Coulomb's Law:

Force on q₂ due to q₁:

$$\vec{F_{12}} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \hat{r_{12}}$$

where r̂₁₂ is the unit vector from q₁ toward q₂.

$$\vec{F_{21}} = -\vec{F_{12}}$$

→ This shows Coulomb's Law obeys **Newton's Third Law of Motion**.

---

### Coulomb's Law in a Medium:

$$\boxed{F_{medium} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 K} \cdot \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}}$$

$$F_{medium} = \frac{F_{air}}{K}$$

- K = Dielectric constant (also called relative permittivity εᵣ)
- K is always ≥ 1 for any medium
- Force in a medium is always **less than or equal to** force in vacuum
- In Air/Vacuum: K = 1 → Force is **maximum**
- In Water: K ≈ 80 → Force is **80 times less** than in air

Also: **ε = ε₀ × εᵣ = ε₀ × K**

---

### Important Characteristics of Coulomb's Force:

1. **Central Force** — Acts along the straight line joining the two charges
2. **Inverse Square Law** — F ∝ 1/r²
3. **Attractive or Repulsive** — Depends on the nature of charges
4. **Conservative Force** — Work done does not depend on the path taken
5. **Newton's Third Law** — F₁₂ = −F₂₁
6. **Medium Dependent** — Force decreases as K increases
7. **Much Stronger than Gravity** — About **10³⁶ times stronger** than gravitational force between two electrons

---

## Comparison: Coulomb's Force vs Gravitational Force

| Property | Coulomb's Force | Gravitational Force |
|----------|----------------|---------------------|
| Acts between | Charges | Masses |
| Nature | Attractive or Repulsive | Always Attractive only |
| Depends on medium | Yes (depends on K) | No |
| Follows inverse square law | Yes | Yes |
| Long range force | Yes | Yes |
| Conservative force | Yes | Yes |
| Relative strength | ~10³⁶ times stronger | Much weaker |

---

## Superposition Principle

### Statement:
The total electrostatic force on a charge due to many other charges is the **vector sum** of the individual forces that each charge exerts on it independently. The presence of other charges does **not affect** the force between any two particular charges.

### Mathematical Form:

$$\vec{F} = \vec{F_1} + \vec{F_2} + \vec{F_3} + ... + \vec{F_n}$$

**Method:** First calculate each force separately using Coulomb's Law, then add all forces vectorially to get the net force.

## 7.1 What is Electric Field? (Definition)

> **"The electric field at a point in space is defined as the electrostatic force experienced by a unit positive test charge placed at that point, without disturbing the source charge."**

### 📐 Mathematical Formula:

$$\boxed{\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q_0}}$$

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **E** | Electric Field at a point |
| **F** | Electrostatic force on the test charge |
| **q₀** | Test charge (positive, very small) |

### ⚠️ Why is the test charge kept very small (q₀ → 0)?
- A large test charge would create its own electric field.
- This would disturb the original electric field we are trying to measure.
- So, the test charge must be **infinitesimally small** so that it does **not disturb the source field**.

### 📝 Correct Definition (Board Exam Language):
> "Electric field **E** at a point is the force per unit positive test charge placed at that point, in the limit when the test charge tends to zero."

$$\boxed{\vec{E} = \lim_{q_0 \to 0} \frac{\vec{F}}{q_0}}$$

---

## 7.2 SI Unit and Dimensional Formula of Electric Field ⭐

### SI Unit:

$$\text{Unit of } E = \frac{\text{Unit of Force}}{\text{Unit of Charge}} = \frac{N}{C} = \text{N/C}$$

> **SI Unit = N/C (Newton per Coulomb)**
> This is also equal to **V/m (Volt per metre)** — both are correct.

| Unit | Full Form | When Used |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| N/C | Newton per Coulomb | Standard SI unit |
| V/m | Volt per metre | Equivalent unit (used in electrostatics & circuits) |

### ✅ Dimensional Formula:

We know:
- Force → [M L T⁻²]
- Charge → [A T] (since Q = I × t, and current I has dimension A)

$$[E] = \frac{[F]}{[Q]} = \frac{[M\ L\ T^{-2}]}{[A\ T]} = \boxed{[M\ L\ T^{-3}\ A^{-1}]}$$

> **Dimensional Formula of Electric Field = [M L T⁻³ A⁻¹]**

---

## 7.3 Electric Field Due to a Point Charge ⭐⭐ (Board Derivation — 3 or 5 Marks)

### 🔹 Setup:
- Let a point charge **+Q** be placed at origin O.
- We want to find the electric field **E** at a point **P**, which is at a distance **r** from Q.
- Place a small positive test charge **q₀** at point P.

### 🔹 Step-by-Step Derivation:

**Step 1:** By Coulomb's Law, the force on test charge q₀ due to source charge Q is:

$$F = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{Q \cdot q_0}{r^2}$$

**Step 2:** Electric field E = Force per unit test charge:

$$E = \frac{F}{q_0} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{Q \cdot q_0}{r^2 \cdot q_0}$$

$$\boxed{E = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{Q}{r^2}}$$

**Step 3:** In vector form (direction matters):

$$\boxed{\vec{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{Q}{r^2}\ \hat{r}}$$

where **r̂** is the unit vector pointing **from Q toward P** (i.e., away from the source charge).

---

### 🔹 Important Conclusions from the Formula:

| Observation | Meaning |
|-------------|---------|
| E ∝ Q | Field is directly proportional to source charge |
| E ∝ 1/r² | As distance increases, field decreases (Inverse Square Law) |
| E depends on direction | It is a vector quantity |
| For **+Q** | E points **away** from Q (outward direction) |
| For **−Q** | E points **toward** Q (inward direction) |

---

### 🔹 Electric Field Diagram (Direction):

```
For Positive Charge (+Q):          For Negative Charge (-Q):

    P ←——— E ←——— +Q               -Q ———→ E ———→ P

   (E points AWAY from +Q)         (E points TOWARD -Q)
```

---

## 7.4 Electric Field is a Vector Quantity

- Electric field has both **magnitude** and **direction**.
- Direction = direction of force on a **positive** test charge.
- For **negative source charge**, E points **toward** the charge.
- For **positive source charge**, E points **away** from the charge.

---

## 7.5 Electric Field Due to Multiple Charges — Superposition Principle

> **"The total electric field at a point due to many charges is the vector sum of the electric fields due to each individual charge."**

$$\vec{E}_{total} = \vec{E_1} + \vec{E_2} + \vec{E_3} + ... + \vec{E_n}$$

- Each field is calculated separately using:

$$E_i = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{Q_i}{r_i^2}$$

- Then all fields are added **vectorially** (direction must be considered).

---

## 7.6 Summary — All Electric Field Formulas

| Formula | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| E = F/q₀ | Definition of Electric Field |
| E = kQ/r² | Field due to a point charge in vacuum |
| E = (1/4πε₀) × Q/r² | Same formula with ε₀ |
| Unit: N/C = V/m | SI Unit of Electric Field |
| Dimension: [M L T⁻³ A⁻¹] | Dimensional Formula |

# 🔷 SECTION 8 : ELECTRIC FIELD LINES

---

## 8.1 What are Electric Field Lines? (Definition)

> **"Electric field lines are imaginary lines drawn in an electric field such that the tangent drawn at any point on the line gives the direction of the electric field at that point."**

### 📌 Key Points:
- They are **imaginary** (not real physical lines).
- They help us **visualise** the electric field.
- Introduced by **Michael Faraday** (he called them "lines of force").
- Also called **Lines of Force**.

---

## 8.2 Properties of Electric Field Lines ⭐⭐⭐ (Most Important — 3 or 5 Marks in Board)

> **Learn all 7 properties with explanation. This is a very common Board question.**

---

### Property 1 — Direction of Field Lines
- Electric field lines **start (originate) from positive charge** and **end (terminate) at negative charge**.
- If a charge is alone (isolated), field lines go from positive charge to **infinity**, or come from infinity to negative charge.

```
+Q ————————→ ————————→ (goes to infinity)
(infinity) ——————→ ——————→ -Q (comes from infinity)
```

---

### Property 2 — Tangent Gives Direction of E
- The **tangent drawn at any point** on an electric field line gives the **direction of the electric field (E)** at that point.
- This is the very definition of a field line.

---

### Property 3 — Field Lines Never Cross Each Other ⭐
- Two electric field lines **never intersect (cross) each other**.
- **Reason:** At the point of intersection, there would be **two tangents**, meaning the electric field would have **two directions at the same point** — which is physically **impossible**. At any point, E can have only ONE direction.

> **Board Exam Tip:** Always give the reason — "because at the point of intersection there would be two different directions of E, which is impossible."

---

### Property 4 — Density of Lines Represents Strength of Field ⭐
- Where field lines are **close together (crowded/dense)** → Electric field is **stronger**.
- Where field lines are **far apart (sparse)** → Electric field is **weaker**.
- This tells us that the field is **stronger near a charge and weaker far away**.

```
Near charge:  ||| (lines close → E is strong)
Far from charge:  |   |   |  (lines far → E is weak)
```

---

### Property 5 — Field Lines are Always Perpendicular to the Surface of a Conductor
- Electric field lines **emerge from** or **enter into** a conductor's surface **at 90° (perpendicular / normal)**.
- There is **no component of E along the surface** of a conductor (if there were, charges would keep moving, which is impossible in electrostatic equilibrium).

---

### Property 6 — Electric Field Lines Never Form Closed Loops ⭐
- Electrostatic field lines are **open curves** — they never form closed loops.
- They must start at a positive charge and end at a negative charge.

> **IMPORTANT DIFFERENCE for Board:**
> - **Electrostatic field lines** → NEVER form closed loops.
> - **Magnetic field lines** → ALWAYS form closed loops.
> This is a **fundamental difference** between electric and magnetic fields.

---

### Property 7 — Field Lines Emerge/Enter Perpendicular to Surface
- Field lines enter and leave the surface of a charged conductor **perpendicularly (at right angles)**.
- This is related to Property 5.

---

### 📋 Properties — Quick Revision Table

| # | Property | Key Phrase |
|---|----------|------------|
| 1 | Start from +ve, end at −ve | Direction of field lines |
| 2 | Tangent = direction of E | Definition property |
| 3 | Never cross each other | E has only one direction at a point |
| 4 | Density = strength of E | Closer lines = stronger field |
| 5 | Perpendicular to conductor | No tangential component on conductor |
| 6 | No closed loops | Difference from magnetic field lines |
| 7 | Perpendicular at surface | Enter/leave at 90° |

---

## 8.3 Electric Field Line Diagrams ⭐⭐ (Drawing in Board Exam)

### Diagram 1 — Positive Charge (+Q):
```
          ↑
      ↖   |   ↗
    ←  ←  +  →  →
      ↙   |   ↘
          ↓
  (Arrows point AWAY from +Q in all directions)
```

### Diagram 2 — Negative Charge (−Q):
```
          ↓
      ↘   |   ↙
    →  →  −  ←  ←
      ↗   |   ↖
          ↑
  (Arrows point TOWARD −Q from all directions)
```

### Diagram 3 — Two Equal and Opposite Charges (+Q and −Q) — Electric Dipole:
```
    +Q ————→————→————→ −Q
    +Q ————→————→————→ −Q
    +Q ————→————→————→ −Q
   (Lines go from + to − , curve outward at ends)
```
- Field lines go **from +Q to −Q**.
- Lines **curve** outward between the two charges.
- This is the field of an **electric dipole**.

### Diagram 4 — Two Equal Same Charges (+Q and +Q):
```
  +Q  ←←  ●  ←←  +Q
       neutral point
       (in the middle)
```
- Lines from each +Q repel each other.
- There is a **neutral point** in the middle where E = 0.
- No field lines pass through the neutral point.

### Diagram 5 — Uniform Electric Field (Between Parallel Plates):
```
  + + + + + + + +
  →  →  →  →  →     (Equal spacing = Uniform field)
  →  →  →  →  →
  →  →  →  →  →
  − − − − − − − −
```
- Field lines are **equally spaced and parallel** → Uniform electric field.
- Produced between two large parallel plates (one +ve, one −ve).

---

## 8.4 Uniform Electric Field ⭐

> **"A uniform electric field is one in which the magnitude and direction of the electric field are the same at every point."**

### Characteristics:
- **Equal spacing** between field lines.
- **All lines are parallel** to each other.
- **Same direction** everywhere.
- **Same magnitude (E)** at every point.
- **Example:** Electric field between two large, parallel, oppositely charged plates.

```
  ++ ++ ++ ++ ++        (Positive plate)
  ————————————————
  →  →  →  →  →        (Uniform field lines)
  →  →  →  →  →
  ————————————————
  −− −− −− −− −−        (Negative plate)
```

---

## 8.5 Non-Uniform Electric Field

- If the field lines are **not equally spaced or not parallel**, the field is **non-uniform**.
- Near a **point charge**, the field is non-uniform (E ∝ 1/r², so it changes with distance).

## Difference: Electric Field Lines vs Magnetic Field Lines

| Property | Electric Field Lines | Magnetic Field Lines |
|----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Source | Electric charges | Magnetic poles / Current |
| Start & End | Start at +ve, end at −ve | Form closed loops (no start/end) |

| Cross each other? | Never | Never |
| Isolated source possible? | Yes (single charge) | No (monopoles don't exist) |

## Difference: Uniform vs Non-Uniform Electric Field

| Property | Uniform Electric Field | Non-Uniform Electric Field |
|----------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| Field lines | Parallel and equally spaced | Not parallel or unequally spaced |
| Magnitude of E | Same everywhere | Different at different points |
| Direction of E | Same everywhere | May vary |
| Example | Between large parallel plates | Near a point charge |

# 🔷 SECTION 11 : BOARD EXAM QUESTIONS (Solved)

---

## 1-Mark Questions

**Q1. What is the SI unit of electric field?**
> **Ans.** N/C (Newton per Coulomb) or V/m (Volt per metre).

**Q2. Write the dimensional formula of electric field.**
> **Ans.** [M L T⁻³ A⁻¹]

**Q3. Why do electric field lines never cross each other?**
> **Ans.** Because at the point of intersection, the electric field would have two directions simultaneously, which is physically impossible. The field can have only one direction at any point.

**Q4. Do electric field lines form closed loops?**
> **Ans.** No. Electrostatic field lines never form closed loops. They always start from positive charge and end at negative charge. (Note: Magnetic field lines do form closed loops.)

**Q5. What does the density of field lines indicate?**
> **Ans.** The density (closeness) of field lines indicates the magnitude (strength) of the electric field. Denser lines = stronger field; sparse lines = weaker field.

---

## 2-Mark Questions

**Q6. Define electric field. Write its SI unit and dimensional formula.**
> **Ans.**
> **Definition:** The electric field at a point is defined as the force per unit positive test charge placed at that point.
> $$E = \frac{F}{q_0}$$
> **SI Unit:** N/C or V/m
> **Dimensional Formula:** [M L T⁻³ A⁻¹]

**Q7. What is a uniform electric field? Give an example.**
> **Ans.**
> A uniform electric field is one in which the magnitude and direction of the electric field are the same at every point.
> Its field lines are parallel and equally spaced.
> **Example:** The electric field produced between two large parallel plates, one positively charged and one negatively charged.

---

## 3-Mark Questions

**Q8. Write any five properties of electric field lines.**
> **Ans.**
> 1. Electric field lines start from positive charges and end at negative charges.
> 2. The tangent at any point on a field line gives the direction of E at that point.
> 3. Two field lines never cross each other, because E can have only one direction at a point.
> 4. The density of field lines indicates the magnitude of E — denser lines mean stronger field.
> 5. Electric field lines are always perpendicular to the surface of a conductor.
> 6. Electric field lines never form closed loops (unlike magnetic field lines).

# 🔷 SECTION 12 : NUMERICAL PROBLEMS (Board Level)

---

**Problem 1:**
Calculate the electric field at a point 0.3 m away from a charge of 5 × 10⁻⁶ C.
(k = 9 × 10⁹ N m² C⁻²)

**Solution:**
$$E = k \cdot \frac{Q}{r^2} = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{5 \times 10^{-6}}{(0.3)^2}$$

$$E = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{5 \times 10^{-6}}{0.09}$$

$$E = 9 \times 10^9 \times 5.56 \times 10^{-5}$$

$$\boxed{E = 5 \times 10^5 \text{ N/C}}$$

---

**Problem 2:**
A force of 4 × 10⁻³ N acts on a charge of 2 × 10⁻⁶ C. Find the electric field at that point.

**Solution:**
$$E = \frac{F}{q_0} = \frac{4 \times 10^{-3}}{2 \times 10^{-6}} = \boxed{2 \times 10^3 \text{ N/C}}$$

---

**Problem 3:**
At what distance from a charge of 4 × 10⁻⁸ C will the electric field be 900 N/C?

**Solution:**
$$E = k \cdot \frac{Q}{r^2} \Rightarrow r^2 = k \cdot \frac{Q}{E}$$

$$r^2 = 9 \times 10^9 \times \frac{4 \times 10^{-8}}{900} = 9 \times 10^9 \times 4.44 \times 10^{-11}$$

$$r^2 = 0.4 \Rightarrow r = \sqrt{0.4} \approx \boxed{0.632 \text{ m}}$$
