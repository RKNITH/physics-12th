## Electric Charge

- Electric charge is a basic property of matter due to which it experiences a force in an electric field.
- It is a **scalar quantity**.
- Two types of charge — **Positive Charge** and **Negative Charge**
- Proton → Positive charge | Electron → Negative charge
- **Basic Law** → Like charges **repel**, unlike charges **attract**.
- **SI Unit** → Coulomb (C)
- Charge on electron = **−1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C**
- Charge on proton = **+1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C**
- This value is called **elementary charge → e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C**
- **1 Coulomb = charge of 6.25 × 10¹⁸ electrons**

---

## Conductors and Insulators

| Conductor | Insulator |
|-----------|-----------|
| Charge flows easily through it | Charge cannot flow through it |
| Has large number of free electrons | Electrons are tightly bound to atoms |
| Examples: Copper, Silver, Aluminium, Iron | Examples: Glass, Rubber, Plastic, Wood |

---

## Properties of Electric Charge

### 1. Additivity of Charge
- The total charge of a system is the **algebraic sum** of all individual charges.
- Sign (+ or −) must be considered.
- **Example:** Charges +3C, −2C, +1C → Total Q = +3 + (−2) + 1 = **+2 C**

---

### 2. Conservation of Charge
- **Statement:** Electric charge can neither be created nor destroyed. It can only be transferred from one body to another. The total charge of an isolated system always remains constant.

**Example 1 — Glass rod and Silk cloth:**
- Glass rod is rubbed with silk → Glass loses electrons → Glass becomes **+ve**, Silk becomes **−ve**
- Total charge before rubbing = 0, Total charge after rubbing = 0 


---

### 3. Quantisation of Charge
- Charge always exists as an **integer multiple of elementary charge e**.

$$\boxed{Q = ne}$$

where n = 0, ±1, ±2, ±3, ... and e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C

- Charges like 0.5e or 1.3e are **not possible in nature**.

**Examples:**
- Charge of 3 electrons = −3e = −3 × 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ = **−4.8 × 10⁻¹⁹ C**
- Charge of 2 protons = +2e = +2 × 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ = **+3.2 × 10⁻¹⁹ C**



---

## Methods of Charging a Body

### 1. Charging by Friction
- Two bodies are rubbed against each other → electrons transfer from one to the other.
- Body that **loses electrons** → becomes **+ve charged**
- Body that **gains electrons** → becomes **−ve charged**
- Total charge of the system remains zero (only transfer, not creation).
- **Example:** Glass rod rubbed with silk cloth.

---

### 2. Charging by Conduction
- A charged body is brought in **direct physical contact** with a neutral body.
- Charge flows until both bodies reach the same potential.
- **Both bodies acquire the same sign of charge.**
- **Example:** A +ve charged metal sphere is touched to a neutral sphere → neutral sphere also becomes **+ve**.

---

### 3. Charging by Induction
- A charged body is brought **near** a neutral conductor **without touching it**.
- Near side of conductor → acquires **opposite charge**
- Far side of conductor → acquires **same sign of charge**
- **The inducing body does not lose any of its charge.**

**Step-by-step Process:**

| Step | What Happens |
|------|-------------|
| Step 1 | Bring +ve charged rod near neutral metal ball (do not touch) |
| Step 2 | Free electrons in ball move toward rod → Near side: −ve, Far side: +ve |
| Step 3 | Connect far side to earth → free electrons from earth flow into the ball and neutralise the +ve charge on far side |
| Step 4 | Remove ground connection first, then remove the rod |
| Step 5 | Ball is left with a net **−ve charge** |

> **Key Difference to Remember:**
> - Conduction → Both bodies get **same sign** of charge
> - Induction → Body gets **opposite sign** of charge

## Coulomb's Law

### Statement:
The electrostatic force between two stationary point charges is **directly proportional to the product of their magnitudes** and **inversely proportional to the square of the distance** between them. This force acts along the straight line joining the two charges.

### Mathematical Derivation:

Let two point charges q₁ and q₂ be placed at distance r in vacuum.

F ∝ q₁q₂ ...(i)

F ∝ 1/r² ...(ii)

Combining both:

$$\boxed{F = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}}$$

$$\boxed{F = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}}$$

**Values:**
- k = Coulomb's constant = **9 × 10⁹ N m² C⁻²**
- ε₀ = Permittivity of free space = **8.85 × 10⁻¹² C² N⁻¹ m⁻²**

---

### Nature of Force:
- q₁q₂ > 0 (same sign charges) → Force is **Repulsive**
- q₁q₂ < 0 (opposite sign charges) → Force is **Attractive**

---

### Vector Form of Coulomb's Law:

Force on q₂ due to q₁:

$$\vec{F_{12}} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \hat{r_{12}}$$

where r̂₁₂ is the unit vector from q₁ toward q₂.

$$\vec{F_{21}} = -\vec{F_{12}}$$

→ This shows Coulomb's Law obeys **Newton's Third Law of Motion**.

---

### Coulomb's Law in a Medium:

$$\boxed{F_{medium} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 K} \cdot \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}}$$

$$F_{medium} = \frac{F_{air}}{K}$$

- K = Dielectric constant (also called relative permittivity εᵣ)
- K is always ≥ 1 for any medium
- Force in a medium is always **less than or equal to** force in vacuum
- In Air/Vacuum: K = 1 → Force is **maximum**
- In Water: K ≈ 80 → Force is **80 times less** than in air

Also: **ε = ε₀ × εᵣ = ε₀ × K**

---

### Important Characteristics of Coulomb's Force:

1. **Central Force** — Acts along the straight line joining the two charges
2. **Inverse Square Law** — F ∝ 1/r²
3. **Attractive or Repulsive** — Depends on the nature of charges
4. **Conservative Force** — Work done does not depend on the path taken
5. **Newton's Third Law** — F₁₂ = −F₂₁
6. **Medium Dependent** — Force decreases as K increases
7. **Much Stronger than Gravity** — About **10³⁶ times stronger** than gravitational force between two electrons

---

## Comparison: Coulomb's Force vs Gravitational Force

| Property | Coulomb's Force | Gravitational Force |
|----------|----------------|---------------------|
| Acts between | Charges | Masses |
| Nature | Attractive or Repulsive | Always Attractive only |
| Depends on medium | Yes (depends on K) | No |
| Follows inverse square law | Yes | Yes |
| Long range force | Yes | Yes |
| Conservative force | Yes | Yes |
| Relative strength | ~10³⁶ times stronger | Much weaker |

## Superposition Principle

### Statement:
The total electrostatic force on a charge due to many other charges is the **vector sum** of the individual forces that each charge exerts on it independently. The presence of other charges does **not affect** the force between any two particular charges.

### Mathematical Form:

$$\vec{F} = \vec{F_1} + \vec{F_2} + \vec{F_3} + ... + \vec{F_n}$$

**Method:** First calculate each force separately using Coulomb's Law, then add all forces vectorially to get the net force.
